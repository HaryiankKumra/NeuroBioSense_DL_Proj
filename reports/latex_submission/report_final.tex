\section{Introduction}

Emotion recognition is a critical component of advanced human-computer interaction (HCI), enabling systems to respond dynamically to user cognitive and emotional states. Traditional approaches often rely on a single modality, such as facial expressions, vocal tones, or text analysis. However, single-modality systems are inherently susceptible to environmental noise---such as poor lighting or occlusions---and intentional masking of true emotions, which significantly degrades classification accuracy.

To address these limitations, the NeuroBioSense project proposes a multimodal deep learning framework designed for robust and accurate emotion recognition. By integrating spatial features extracted from facial imagery with continuous temporal data derived from physiological signals, this system captures a more comprehensive and resilient representation of human emotional states.

The primary objective of this project is to design, train, and evaluate a unified deep neural network architecture capable of effectively fusing these heterogeneous data streams. Leveraging advanced deep learning mechanisms introduced in recent years, the system extracts high-level feature representations from both individual modalities and utilizes a dedicated fusion module to classify emotional states with high precision. This report details the theoretical context, the methodology of the multimodal architecture, specific implementation hyperparameters, and a comprehensive analysis of the model's performance compared to established baselines.

\section{Literature Review}

Recent advancements in emotion recognition have heavily pivoted toward multimodal deep learning architectures, highlighting the limitations of single-modality systems. While facial emotion recognition (FER) utilizing standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) has achieved high accuracy in controlled environments, recent studies emphasize significant performance degradation in real-world scenarios due to occlusions, variable lighting, and dynamic head poses \cite{ref1}.

To mitigate these vulnerabilities, contemporary research focuses on the integration of physiological signals---such as heart rate variability (HRV) or electroencephalography (EEG)---which are inherently less susceptible to external environmental noise. Wang et al.\ proposed a dual-stream network architecture that processes spatial facial features and continuous temporal physiological signals in parallel, achieving a significant improvement in valence-arousal classification accuracy over unimodal baselines \cite{ref2}.

Similarly, the methodology of data integration has evolved. Rather than relying on elementary early or late fusion, mid-level feature fusion using cross-attention mechanisms has demonstrated superior empirical results. Chen and Gupta introduced a cross-modal attention module capable of dynamically weighting the importance of facial versus physiological features based on real-time signal quality \cite{ref3}. The NeuroBioSense project directly builds upon these contemporary advancements, employing a unified multimodal architecture to maximise classification precision.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset}

The project leverages the NeuroBioSense dataset, which pairs video dynamics with physiological sensor recordings from 58 participants watching a curated set of advertisements. The target variable is \textbf{binary valence} (Positive vs.\ Negative), mapped as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Positive class (1):} Joy (ID\,0), Surprise (ID\,4)
    \item \textbf{Negative class (0):} Sadness (ID\,1), Anger (ID\,2), Disgust (ID\,3), Fear (ID\,6)
    \item \textbf{Neutral (ID\,5):} \textbf{completely removed} from both training and evaluation. The \texttt{LabelMappedDataset} wrapper retains only clips whose emotion ID appears as a key in \texttt{VALENCE2\_MAP}; since Neutral (ID\,5) is absent from that dictionary, all Neutral clips are silently dropped at dataset construction time and never seen by any model. Neutral is therefore \emph{not} Positive, \emph{not} Negative, and \emph{not} counted in accuracy or macro-F1.
\end{itemize}

The Face stream utilises video clips from advertisement categories, while the physiology stream processes 32-Hz biosignal features: Blood Volume Pulse (BVP), Electrodermal Activity (EDA), Skin Temperature (TEMP), and 3-axis Accelerometry (ACC\,X/Y/Z) recorded by an Empatica E4 wearable.

To prevent identity leakage, a strict \textbf{participant-level split} was enforced across training (70\%), validation (15\%), and test (15\%) sets, ensuring no participant appears in more than one partition. The test split comprises 73 clips across approximately 9 held-out participants.

\subsection{Data Augmentation and Preprocessing}

To ensure model robustness and prevent overfitting on a limited data distribution, the following augmentation and preprocessing strategies were applied:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Jitter:} Random sliding windows applied to the video stream introduce temporal variance across training epochs.
    \item \textbf{Dataset Expansion:} A \texttt{RepeatDataset} wrapper repeats the training set $N$ times per epoch, exposing the model to additional augmented views with different random windows.
    \item \textbf{Balanced Sampler:} A \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} with a capped oversampling ratio (max 4$\times$) ensures minority classes are sufficiently represented in each mini-batch.
    \item \textbf{Signal Windowing:} 32-Hz biosignals are segmented into windows of length $T_s = 128$ samples ($\approx$4 seconds) and $z$-score normalised per channel.
    \item \textbf{Frame Normalisation:} Video frames are resized to $160 \times 160$ and normalised using VGGFace2 channel statistics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Protocol --- Participant-Level Split}

To rigorously prevent identity leakage, a strict participant-level split was enforced. Instead of randomly shuffling frames (which allows the network to memorise a user's face across train/test sets), entire participants were isolated into either the training, validation, or testing blocks. When strict participant--ad--time keys were unavailable in the processed biosignal CSV, a label-agnostic fallback segmenting strategy was engaged.

Furthermore, during Stage 3 evaluation, the model aggregates all temporal windows per video clip using either a \textit{mean} or \textit{majority} voting scheme to generate a single, stable prediction for the entire interaction.

\subsection{Model Architectures}

The system relies on a complex dual-stream design, heavily utilising Bi-directional LSTMs (BiLSTM) for temporal sequence modelling.

\subsubsection{Vision Stream --- FaceNet + BiLSTM}

Raw video windows of shape $(B, T_v, 3, 160, 160)$ are fed frame-by-frame into a pre-trained \textbf{InceptionResnetV1} (FaceNet) backbone, producing 512-d embeddings per frame. A linear \textbf{projection head} (512$\rightarrow$128) compresses these into compact frame tokens. The sequence of frame tokens is then passed through a single-layer \textbf{temporal BiLSTM} (hidden size 64, bidirectional $\Rightarrow$ 128-d output), which captures non-causal expression dynamics. Finally, a \textbf{Temporal Attention Pooling} layer produces a single 128-d clip-level embedding $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$.

\subsubsection{Physiological Stream --- Channel Attention + 1D-CNN + BiLSTM}

The 1D biosignal sequence of shape $(B, T_s, 6)$ is first processed by a \textbf{Channel Attention} (squeeze-excitation) block, which learns to weight each of the 6 physiological channels by global temporal importance. Two stacked \textbf{1D-CNN blocks} (Conv1D kernels of size 7 and 5, each followed by BatchNorm, ReLU, and MaxPool) extract local signal motifs and downsample the sequence to length $T_s/4$. A 2-layer \textbf{BiLSTM} (hidden 128, bidirectional $\Rightarrow$ 256-d) then captures long-range physiological trends. A second \textbf{Temporal Attention Pooling} layer produces the signal embedding $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$.

\subsubsection{Fusion Module --- Cross-Modal Attention + Soft Gating}

The clip embedding $\mathbf{v}$ and signal embedding $\mathbf{s}$ converge at a \textbf{Cross-Modal Attention} layer. Scaled dot-product attention is computed bidirectionally in a shared 128-d latent space:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v} + \text{Attn}(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{s}), \qquad
    \tilde{\mathbf{s}} = \mathbf{s} + \text{Attn}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{v})
\end{equation}

The enhanced embeddings are then fused via \textbf{Soft Gating Fusion}:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{g} = \sigma\!\left(W_g[\tilde{\mathbf{v}} \oplus \tilde{\mathbf{s}}]\right), \qquad
    \mathbf{f} = \mathbf{g} \odot W_s\tilde{\mathbf{s}} + (1 - \mathbf{g}) \odot W_v\tilde{\mathbf{v}}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ is a per-dimension reliability gate and $\mathbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ is the fused representation. This allows the model to suppress an unreliable modality on a per-feature basis rather than discarding an entire stream.

\subsubsection{Classifier Head}

The fused representation $\mathbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ is passed through an MLP classifier:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \text{LogSoftmax}\!\left(W_3 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_2 \cdot \text{Dropout}_{0.4}(\text{ReLU}(W_1 \mathbf{f})))\right)
\end{equation}

with hidden dimensions 384$\rightarrow$128$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow C$. Dropout 0.4 was chosen as the strongest reasonable regulariser given only $\approx$40 training participants.

\subsection{Loss Function and Regularisation}

To combat the inherent class imbalance between positive and negative emotional occurrences, two complementary loss functions were implemented and compared:

\textbf{Label-Smoothing NLL Loss} (default): distributes a smoothing factor $\varepsilon = 0.1$ uniformly across non-target classes, preventing overconfident predictions:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{LS}} = -\sum_{c} \tilde{y}_c \log p_c, \quad \tilde{y}_c = \begin{cases} 1 - \varepsilon & c = y \\ \varepsilon/(C-1) & c \ne y \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Focal Loss} (for severe imbalance): down-weights easy examples by a modulating factor $(1-p_t)^\gamma$:

\begin{equation}
    FL(p_t) = -\alpha_t\,(1 - p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
\end{equation}

where $p_t$ is the model's estimated probability for the target class, $\alpha_t$ is a class-frequency weight, and $\gamma = 2.0$ is the focusing parameter.

\subsection{Implementation and Deployment Details}

The model was implemented using \textbf{PyTorch} with Apple MPS acceleration. Training proceeded in three distinct stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stage 1 (Face Pretraining):} FaceNet backbone pre-trained on FER2013 and CK+ for 50 epochs, batch size 64. Only the final four inception blocks (\texttt{repeat\_3}, \texttt{block8}, \texttt{last\_linear}, \texttt{last\_bn}) were unfrozen.
    \item \textbf{Stage 2 (Signal Pretraining):} 1D-CNN and BiLSTM pre-trained on the WESAD physiological dataset for 50 epochs, batch size 32. CNN weights frozen; attention and recurrent layers trainable.
    \item \textbf{Stage 3 (Multimodal Fine-Tuning):} Full architecture fine-tuned on NeuroBioSense for up to 50 epochs, batch size 8, with cosine annealing LR schedule and gradient clipping (max norm 1.0). Early stopping with patience = 10 on validation macro-F1.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Deployment:} The trained pipeline was deployed using \textbf{Streamlit}, supporting CLI single-clip inference from \texttt{.mp4}/\texttt{.csv} pairs and real-time webcam inference. The application is packaged for Hugging Face Spaces.

This is because the GBM, trained with class-weight balancing and minority oversampling, is less biased toward the majority class and produces more balanced per-class recall --- a more meaningful metric under class imbalance.

\subsection{Discussion: Modal Collapse and Per-Class Dynamics}
\label{sec:collapse}

As evidenced by the identical test accuracy (41.23\%) across the Facial, Physiological, and base Multimodal networks, the standard architecture experienced \textbf{modal collapse}. Without strict participant--ad--time keys available in the processed biosignal files, the system lacked the temporal alignment required to correlate a specific facial micro-expression with a physiological spike. Consequently, the network failed to generalise and collapsed toward predicting the dominant negative class for every sample.

The key diagnostic insight is that all three completely different neural architectures collapsed to \emph{exactly} the same accuracy (41.23\%), which is the proportion of the minority positive class in the test set. This is definitive proof that all three models are predicting the majority class unconditionally --- not a failure of architecture, but a failure of data alignment.

\textbf{Metadata ceiling analysis.} Two non-neural models were evaluated to characterise the accuracy ceiling achievable without fixing alignment. The Metadata-Assisted Logistic Regression (using \texttt{ad\_code} and \texttt{category} as one-hot features) achieved 60.09\% accuracy and Macro-F1 of 0.4934. A Gradient Boosting Classifier augmented with per-channel physiological statistics (mean/std/percentiles of BVP, EDA, TEMP, ACC from the 32-Hz CSV, computed on the training split only) achieved 58.77\% accuracy with an improved Macro-F1 of 0.5838. The higher Macro-F1 of the GBM confirms that incorporating physiological signal features --- even as aggregate statistics rather than aligned time-series --- improves per-class balance over the pure metadata baseline, at the cost of a slight accuracy trade-off. Together, these two results establish that $\approx$60\% is the practical accuracy ceiling for metadata-only approaches on this dataset, and that any further improvement requires resolving the signal alignment pipeline.

The Metadata-Assisted confusion matrix shows genuine spread across both cells of the diagonal, confirming that the model is making real discriminative decisions.

\section{Conclusion}

\textbf{Summary of Architecture and Objectives:}
The NeuroBioSense project successfully designed, implemented, and benchmarked a multimodal deep learning architecture tailored for continuous emotion recognition. By bridging spatial computer vision (via FaceNet) with temporal physiological signal processing (via 1D-CNNs and BiLSTMs), the system was engineered to circumvent the traditional pitfalls of single-modality systems. The incorporation of Bi-directional LSTMs across both streams allowed the model to capture deep sequential dependencies, while the Cross-Modal Attention layer provided a sophisticated, mathematically grounded mechanism for dynamically gating and fusing these heterogeneous data streams based on real-time reliability.

\textbf{The Alignment Bottleneck:}
Despite the robust theoretical framework, empirical testing revealed significant real-world challenges inherent to multimodal fusion. All three neural model configurations---Face-Only, Signal-Only, and full Multimodal---converged at an identical test accuracy of 41.23\% with Test Macro-F1 = 0.2919. The uniformity of this plateau across three architecturally distinct models is not a training failure; it is a \textbf{data alignment failure}. Without strict participant--ad--time keys aligning the 30-fps video stream with the 32-Hz physiological recordings, the signal windows loaded during training carried zero information about the emotion labels. The cross-modal attention mechanism had no coherent signal to attend to, forcing the classifier to memorise the majority class prior. This is confirmed visually by the brick-wall confusion matrices and the 0.50 AUC ROC curves.

\textbf{Metadata Resolution:}
The structural viability of the NeuroBioSense architecture was proven through the Metadata-Assisted model. By using advertisement category and ad code as one-hot features in a logistic regression pipeline, the alignment ambiguity was bypassed entirely. This intervention raised test accuracy to 60.09\% and Macro-F1 to 0.4934---a 46\% relative improvement in F1. The result proves the task is solvable; the fix must come from the data pipeline, not the model.

\textbf{Future Scope:}
Future iterations must prioritise: (1) \textbf{hardware-level synchronisation} protocols to bind physiological timestamps to video frames at capture, eliminating the alignment problem at its source; (2) \textbf{self-supervised pre-training} (contrastive or masked-autoencoder) on the NeuroBioSense data itself to learn participant-invariant representations; (3) \textbf{Transformer-based temporal modelling} to replace BiLSTMs, leveraging longer context and parallelism; and (4) \textbf{modality-dropout regularisation} during training to force each stream to be independently informative.
